About

I am a Computational Social Science M.S. student at the University of California, San Diego with a background in psychology and communication. My work sits at the intersection of machine learning and the behavioral and cognitive sciences, where I use statistical and computational methods to study how models represent information and how their behavior can be measured and evaluated.

My research focuses on the evaluation of modern machine learning models, using representation learning, multimodal models, and large-scale datasets to investigate how models encode semantic information and how representations align across modalities.

More broadly, I am interested in human-centered approaches to machine learning and how careful evaluation, combined with insights from behavioral and cognitive science, can improve our understanding of model behavior and help support the development of more reliable and responsible AI systems.

Evaluating Moral Framing Bias in Large Language Models

Question

Do large language models exhibit the same omission bias observed in human moral psychology, where harmful actions (commissions) are judged as more wrong than equally harmful failures to act (omissions)?

Data

Constructed 14 commission–omission minimal pairs based on scenarios from moral psychology experiments.

Method

Evaluated four autoregressive language models (GPT-2, GPT-2-Medium, GPT-2-Large, GPT-Neo-125M) using next-token prediction to compute the conditional probability of moral evaluation words.

Result

Across models and conditions, LLMs did not reproduce the human omission bias.
